## The Foundation

God narrows his focus to widen his reach. After the tower of Babel scatters the nations, God calls one man — Abram, a pagan from Ur of the Chaldees — and makes him a promise so extravagant it would take the rest of the Bible to fulfill: "I will make of you a great nation, and I will bless you and make your name great, so that you will be a blessing... and in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed" (Genesis 12:1-3). This is the covenant that gives the Bible its trajectory. Everything that follows — the patriarchs, the exodus, the monarchy, the prophets, the exile, the return, and the coming of Christ — is the working out of what God promised to Abraham under the stars.

The covenant is confirmed and deepened in stages. In Genesis 15, God passes between the halved animals alone — a unilateral oath, staking his own existence on his faithfulness. In Genesis 17, circumcision becomes the covenant sign, and Abram becomes Abraham, "father of a multitude." In Genesis 22, on Mount Moriah, Abraham raises the knife over Isaac — the child of the promise — and God provides a ram in his place. The author of Hebrews will later say that Abraham received Isaac back "as a figure" of resurrection (Hebrews 11:19). The mountain where the ram died in Isaac's place is tradition's site of the future temple — and of Golgotha itself.

## Key Old Testament Passages

| Passage | Significance |
|---------|-------------|
| Genesis 12:1-3 | The call and the promise — all families of the earth blessed |
| Genesis 15:1-21 | The covenant ceremony — God's unilateral oath |
| Genesis 17:1-14 | Circumcision as the covenant sign; the promise of Isaac |
| Genesis 22:1-19 | The binding of Isaac — God will provide the lamb |
| Psalm 72:17 | "May all nations be blessed through him" |
| Isaiah 51:1-2 | "Look to Abraham... I called him as one, and I blessed him and multiplied him" |

## Looking Ahead

The promise to Abraham was never limited to one nation. "All the families of the earth" was always the scope. In Revelation 7:9, John sees the fulfillment: "a great multitude that no one could number, from every nation, from all tribes and peoples and languages, standing before the throne." The blessing promised to one man under the stars has become an uncountable multitude worshiping the Lamb. Abraham's faith was not misplaced. It was simply ahead of its time.
