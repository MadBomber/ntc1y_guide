## The Foundation

God makes a promise to a shepherd-king that will echo through a thousand years of prophecy and find its fulfillment in a carpenter's son from Nazareth. In 2 Samuel 7, David wants to build a house for God. God's response turns the offer inside out: it is not David who will build a house for God, but God who will build a house — a dynasty — for David. "Your house and your kingdom shall be made sure forever before me. Your throne shall be established forever" (2 Samuel 7:16). This is the Davidic covenant, and it reshapes everything Israel hopes for.

From this point on, the prophets look forward to a king from David's line who will reign with justice and righteousness, who will shepherd God's people, and whose kingdom will have no end. When the monarchy crumbles and Israel goes into exile, the promise does not die — it intensifies. Isaiah sees a shoot springing from the stump of Jesse. Jeremiah promises a righteous Branch. Ezekiel envisions one shepherd, "my servant David," who will feed the flock. Daniel sees "one like a son of man" receiving dominion and glory and a kingdom that will never be destroyed. The worse things get, the more Israel clings to the hope that God will keep his word to David.

By the first century, the expectation of a Davidic king — the Messiah, the "anointed one" — had become the burning center of Jewish hope. But the king who came was not the king they expected.

## Looking Ahead

The throne of David is not empty. The New Testament declares that Jesus is already reigning at the Father's right hand (Acts 2:33-36, Hebrews 1:3). But his reign is not yet fully visible. The day is coming when "the kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord and of his Christ, and he shall reign forever and ever" (Revelation 11:15). The king who entered Jerusalem on a donkey will return — and every knee will bow.
