## The Foundation

The world God made good has gone terribly wrong. By Genesis 6, human wickedness has become so pervasive that "every intention of the thoughts of his heart was only evil continually." God's response is devastating and merciful at the same time: he will judge the world with a flood, but he will save one family through an ark. Noah — a righteous man in a corrupt generation — is commanded to build a vessel of salvation, and through that vessel God preserves not only humanity but the entire created order. When the waters recede, God makes a covenant with Noah, with his descendants, and with every living creature: never again will a flood destroy the earth. The rainbow becomes the sign of a promise that rests entirely on God's faithfulness, not on humanity's goodness.

The pattern established here will repeat throughout the Bible: judgment and salvation are not opposites but companions. The same God who sends the flood provides the ark. The same waters that destroy the wicked carry the righteous to safety. This is the first covenant in Scripture that uses the word *berith* — and it is universal in scope, encompassing all of creation. It establishes God's commitment to preserving the world long enough for his redemptive plan to unfold.

## Key Old Testament Passages

| Passage | Significance |
|---------|-------------|
| Genesis 6:5-8 | The corruption of humanity and Noah finding grace |
| Genesis 6:14-22 | The ark — God's provision of salvation through judgment |
| Genesis 8:20-22 | Noah's sacrifice and God's promise to sustain the earth |
| Genesis 9:8-17 | The covenant with all living creatures — the rainbow sign |
| Isaiah 54:9-10 | God invokes the Noahic covenant as a picture of his unfailing love |

## Fulfilled in Christ

| New Testament | Connection |
|---------------|------------|
| 1 Peter 3:18-22 | The flood as a type of baptism — saved through water by the resurrection of Christ |
| 2 Peter 2:5 | Noah as a "herald of righteousness" — a preacher preserved through judgment |
| 2 Peter 3:5-7 | The flood as a pattern for the final judgment by fire |
| Matthew 24:37-39 | "As were the days of Noah, so will be the coming of the Son of Man" |
| Hebrews 11:7 | Noah's faith in building the ark condemned the world and secured righteousness |
| Luke 17:26-27 | Jesus uses the days of Noah as a warning about readiness |

## Looking Ahead

The Noahic covenant guarantees that God will preserve the world — but not forever in its fallen state. Peter draws a direct line from the flood to the final day when "the heavens will pass away with a roar, and the heavenly bodies will be burned up and dissolved" (2 Peter 3:10). Yet as with Noah, judgment is not the last word. What follows is "new heavens and a new earth in which righteousness dwells." The ark pointed to Christ. The rainbow points to the promise that God's mercy outlasts his wrath.
