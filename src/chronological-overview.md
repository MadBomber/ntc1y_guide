## The New Testament Is Not Arranged Chronologically

Most people assume the New Testament is in chronological order. It is not. The traditional arrangement follows a topical logic:

- **Gospels** (Matthew, Mark, Luke, John) — four accounts of Jesus' life
- **History** (Acts) — the early church
- **Paul's Letters** (Romans through Philemon) — longest to shortest
- **General Letters** (Hebrews through Jude) — various authors
- **Prophecy** (Revelation) — the end of the age

This arrangement has served the church well for centuries. But it creates some odd disconnections. You read all four Gospel accounts separately rather than seeing how they interweave. You read Romans as if it were Paul's first letter, when it was actually written late in his ministry. You read James without knowing where it fits in the early church's development.

## What Chronological Order Does

A chronological reading reassembles the story as it happened:

### The Gospels Are Harmonized

Rather than reading Matthew 1-28, then Mark 1-16, then Luke 1-24, then John 1-21, this study weaves the four accounts together event by event. When Jesus feeds the five thousand, you read the accounts in Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John in close succession — seeing how each author emphasizes different details of the same event.

This approach:
- Reduces repetition (you are not reading the same event four times weeks apart)
- Reveals each Gospel writer's unique perspective
- Creates a single coherent narrative of Jesus' life

### Paul's Letters Are Placed in Acts

Paul wrote his letters during the events described in Acts. In this study, you read each letter at the point in Acts when Paul actually wrote it:

| Letter | Written During | Placed After |
|--------|---------------|--------------|
| Galatians | First Missionary Journey | Acts 14 |
| 1 & 2 Thessalonians | Second Journey, from Corinth | Acts 18:1-17 |
| 1 & 2 Corinthians | Third Journey, from Ephesus/Macedonia | Acts 19-20 |
| Romans | Third Journey, from Corinth | Acts 20:1-6 |
| Ephesians, Philippians, Colossians, Philemon | First Roman Imprisonment | Acts 28 |
| 1 Timothy, Titus | Between imprisonments | After Acts |
| 2 Timothy | Second Roman Imprisonment | After 1 Timothy/Titus |

This means you understand what Paul was dealing with when he wrote each letter. When you read 1 Corinthians, you already know what was happening in Corinth from Acts 18. When you read Philippians, you know Paul is chained to a Roman guard from Acts 28.

### James Fits Into the Early Church

The letter of James was likely the earliest New Testament document, written to Jewish believers in the early 40s AD. In this study, it appears after Acts 8 — when persecution scattered Jewish believers throughout the Roman world — which is exactly the audience James addresses.

### The General Letters and Revelation Close the Story

Hebrews, the letters of Peter, John, and Jude, and Revelation are placed in their approximate historical positions in the late first century, creating a natural arc from the early church's explosive growth through its maturation and testing to the apocalyptic hope of Christ's return.

## A Note on Dating

Scholars debate the exact dates of many New Testament writings. The chronological order used in this study follows mainstream evangelical scholarship while acknowledging areas of uncertainty. Where dating is debated, we have chosen placements that serve the narrative flow and are historically defensible. The goal is not to resolve every dating question but to help you experience the New Testament as a living, unfolding story rather than a static collection of disconnected books.

## The Six Phases

This study divides the New Testament story into six phases that follow its natural narrative arc:

1. **The Coming of Christ** (Weeks 1-4) — From eternity past to the launch of Jesus' public ministry
2. **The Galilean Ministry** (Weeks 5-11) — The height of Jesus' popularity, teaching, and miracle-working in Galilee
3. **The Road to Jerusalem** (Weeks 12-16) — The journey south, growing opposition, and final week teachings
4. **Passion and Resurrection** (Weeks 17-20) — The climax: Last Supper, cross, and empty tomb
5. **The Early Church** (Weeks 21-41) — Pentecost through Paul's imprisonment, with letters integrated
6. **Letters & Revelation** (Weeks 42-52) — Pastoral guidance, general epistles, and the hope of Christ's return
